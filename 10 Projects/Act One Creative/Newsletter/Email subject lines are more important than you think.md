Why Preheaders Matter

Think about it like this: Your subject line is bolded, it grabs their attention, and maybe it has an emoji for more effect. The reader is still on edge, but they notice the preheader gives them a little bit of information, or makes them curious. Like the example above.

The preheader is often the little extra push readers need to open the email.

Together, you've got anywhere from 90 to 140 characters (depending on which email client someone's using) to convince them to open your email instead of the other 146 sitting in their inbox.

Gmail shows about 90 characters on desktop and Apple Mail on iPhone shows around 140. So why would you waste that space when you could
How to Write Good Preheaders

Don't repeat your subject line

If your subject line says "Your free trial expires in 3 days," don't use your preheader to say the exact same thing. Instead, you could go with

> Your free trial expires in 3 days - Here's how to upgrade and save 20% before it's too late

See the difference? The subject line creates urgency, and the preheader gives them something to act on so they don't just let the trial expire.

Add context that didn't fit in the subject line

Your subject line has limited space before it gets cut off. Use the preheader to give them a reason to care:

> 3 changes that doubled our conversion rate - And how you can implement them in under an hour

Include a soft call to action

While your subject line hooks them, your preheader can nudge them toward opening your email:

> Still sending cold emails like it's 2015? - See what's working in 2026 (and what's not)

Watch your length

Aim for 40-130 characters. Any longer and it gets cut off mid-sentence, which looks sloppy.
