Everybody’s a thought leader. Which means nobody is.

I know you’ve heard this time and again, but I’ll say it again: we live in an age of information.

Since the release of tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini, people have been able to push content out like crazy. 

That sounds like a win until you look at your feed, which is probably flooded with content that’s technically correct and structured neatly, but is completely forgettable. 

Research in cognitive psychology shows that people remember information that is novel, emotionally charged, or meaningfully distinct. Familiar patterns are processed faster but retained less. When everything follows the same structure, tone, and cadence, the brain tags it as predictable and moves on.

There’s also growing evidence that audiences can detect AI-assisted writing at rates higher than chance, especially when it lacks specific lived detail or strong point of view. Even when they can’t explicitly label it as AI, they describe it as “generic,” “flat,” or “sounds like LinkedIn.” And trust drops fast when content feels mass-produced.

The result? We’ve optimized for clarity and lost memorability.

Readable is no longer rare. Insight is.

The brands and creators breaking through right now aren’t the ones publishing more. They’re the ones publishing truer. Specific stories. Clear opinions. Hard-won lessons. Language that sounds like a person who has actually been in the arena.

AI can help you draft. It can’t replace your pattern of thought, your taste, or your lived experience.

Originality isn’t a luxury anymore.

It’s the only thing that cuts through.
